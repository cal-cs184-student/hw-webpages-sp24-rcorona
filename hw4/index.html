Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

When the ks constant is very high, the forces maintaining the structural constraints between pairs of springs is higher which causes them to bend and stretch much less as the cloth falls over time. If the ks constant is lower, then the forces from spring constraints is lower, causing the springs to stretch and bend more as the cloth falls. In terms of final resting position, the magnitude of the spring forces will interact differently with gravity (whose magnitude is always the same). Higher spring constants will cause the cloth to remain more rigid/taught against gravity, causing the cloth to sag much less. In contrast, lower spring constants will cause the spring forces to lose out to gravity, causing the cloth to sag and bend much more.

What about for density?

Higher values of density entails higher mass for each point mass and therefore a greater acceleration from gravitational force being enacted on each point. Therefore, higher values will result in both the cloth falling faster (proportional to the increase in mass) as well as will cause the final resting position of the cloth to sag more since the amount of spring force needed to cancel out the gravitational force will higher (and therefore will require longer spring lengths in order to cancel out gravity). Lower density values will cause a lower gravitational acceleration, causing the cloth to fall more slowly. Additionally, the cloth will sag less at its final resting position since the spring force required to cancel it out (and therefore the spring length) will be lower.

What about for damping?

A higher damping value means a higher loss of kinetic energy as the cloth moves. In other words, this means that the velocity of the point masses at any given time step is heavily throttled. Qualitatively, this results in the cloth falling more slowly given it's throttling of velocity. Additionally, once the cloth falls to a point where the springs start to pull back on it, a higher damping value will result in a a lower amount of oscillation as the cloth converges to its final resting position. Conversely, a lower damping value will result in greater velocity values, causing the cloth to fall faster. Once the cloth falls to a point where the springs start to pull back on it, the oscillations before it converges to its final resting spot will not only last longer but will also have a higher magnitude.

Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

A shader program is a program that is run in parallel for individual, independent units of a graphics pipeline (unit here being defined by the type of shader program being run) in order to parallelize the rendering process of a scene. By running a shader program instance in parallel per unit, the efficiency of the rendering pipeline can be greatly increased. Two types of shader programs are vertex shaders and fragment shaders.

Vertex Shaders:Vertex shaders are programs which operate over the vertices of primitives in a scene. Their primary purpose is to (1) determine the screen coordinates of a given vertex during rasterization, and (2) to compute certain vertex properties which may be useful for coloring fragments during fragment shader program execution (i.e. to compute inputs to the fragment shader), such as the vertex normal and uv coordinates in world coordinates. Vertex shaders can also modify the positions and normals of vertices in order to alter the geometry of the scene, as is done in displacement mapping.

Fragment Shaders: Fragment shaders operate over pixels to determine their color in the final rendered image. For example, in the fragment shader, the input normal (which is determined by barycentric coordinate interpolation given vertex normals) at a given screen coordinate can be used to determine the illumination contribution from lights in the scene as well as model their interactions with different material properties (e.g. specular, lambertian), as is done with Blinn-Phong shading. Fragment shaders can also be used to color objects in the scene by sampling from a provided texture, either directly by essentially mapping the texture to the object, or by simulating mirror

In summary, the two types of shaders work together by first having the vertex shader determine the final geometry of the scene (by potentially modifying the scene vertices), as well as determining the world and on-screen coordinates of a given vertex. This information is then passed on to the fragment shader, which uses interpolated vertex information in order to determine the final color of a given pixel in the scene.

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model is a formulation for simulating the illumination contributed by light hitting a surface. It consists of four main components, the emissive, diffuse, ambient, and specular components. Emissive light represents light being emitted by the surface itself, such as when the surface represents a light (the cloth in the simulator has no emissive component). Diffuse light represents light that is scattered uniformly in all directions after hitting the surface, therefore the contribution of this component is independent of viewing direction. However, the intensity of the diffuse light is dependent on the direction of the light relative to the surface normal, and will be attenuated by the angle between the normal and the light direction based on Lambert's cosine law. Ambient light is meant to simulate contributions from indirect or global lighting in the scene, since shaders cannot simulate the dynamics of light bouncing around a scene as is done in ray tracing. Ambient light is not affected by viewing direction or by the direction of any light. Specular light represents light that is reflected in reflective direction from the light source, and is meant to simulate how reflective a material is. The exponent parameter in the specular light component formula can be used to tune the effective solid angle through which light is scattered around the reflective direction. The intensity of the specular component is also affected by Lambert's Cosine Law, but instead of computing the angle between the surface normal and the light direction, the angle is computed between the normal and the bisecting vector between the viewing direction and the light direction (this is done in part to avoid numerical issues with angles greater than 90 degrees which would drive the specular component to 0 if computing the dot product between the reflectance direction and the viewing direction as is done in Phong shading).
